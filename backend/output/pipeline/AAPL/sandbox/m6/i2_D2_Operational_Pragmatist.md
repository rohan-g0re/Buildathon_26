# Initial Counterpoints: Three Critical Weaknesses

**1. The Services Math Doesn't Close the Margin Gap — It's Wishful Thinking**

The proposal assumes $8–12 annual Services revenue per SE Plus device justifies an 8–12% margin compression on hardware. Let's be precise: if Apple sells 25M SE Plus units at $650 with 18% gross margin instead of 45% on a $799 flagship, that's a loss of ~$4.3B in gross profit annually. The Services offset claims $1.6–2.4B incremental revenue—but this isn't incremental *profit*, it's incremental *revenue*. Services gross margins are high (~70%), so you're looking at ~$1.1–1.7B in incremental gross profit. The math leaves a $2.6–3.2B gap that the proposal never addresses. The mitigation (aggressive bundling of Apple One) doesn't solve this; it just front-loads Services revenue while cannibalizing future subscriptions. Where's the actual profit recovery?

**2. The Cannibalization Assumption Is Dangerously Optimistic**

The proposal claims strict channel discipline (carrier partnerships, online-only) prevents cannibalization, but this ignores consumer behavior. In China, price-sensitive buyers who were considering a $799 iPhone 16 *will* buy the $649 SE Plus instead—that's not channel discipline, that's basic economics. The 15M unit cannibalization estimate in the downside section feels like a floor, not a realistic scenario. Worse, the proposal doesn't address that Apple's Chinese retail footprint is small relative to competitors; carrier partnerships are notoriously difficult to control (carriers will push SE Plus because it's higher-margin for them), and online sales in China face intense logistics competition. The channel control mechanism simply won't work as described.

**3. You're Betting Huawei Will Quit, Not That You'll Win**

The proposal's core claim is that Apple's superior cost structure means it can "sustain a price war longer." But Huawei isn't a profit-maximizing rational actor—it's a state-backed entity with geopolitical objectives and access to subsidized capital. The U.S. sanctions have actually *strengthened* Huawei's domestic positioning by making it a nationalist symbol. Competing on price against a subsidized competitor in a nationalist market is a losing game, and the 2-year exit clause doesn't help—by then, Huawei has consolidated 25%+ share, and Apple's brand positioning in China is permanently damaged. You're not defending market share; you're entering a war you can't win, just at lower margins.
